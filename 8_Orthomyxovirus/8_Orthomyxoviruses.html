Pathogenesis & Immunity

  • Tropism and damage
    • Infects upper and lower respiratory epithelium ‚Üí epithelial death and impaired mucociliary clearance
  • Innate immunity
    • Early control by type I IFNs, macrophages, and NK cells; cytokines cause systemic symptoms
  • Adaptive immunity
    • CD8+ T cells clear infected cells; neutralizing anti‚ÄëHA/NA antibodies block infection
  • Complications
    • Excessive inflammation worsens lung injury; epithelial damage predisposes to secondary bacterial infection (e.g., S. pneumoniae, S. aureus).
  • Antigenic variation
    • Drift ‚Üí seasonal epidemics; shift ‚Üí potential pandemics ‚Üí vaccines require periodic updates

Epidemiology

  • Viral/host: Influenza A ‚Äî multiple hosts and pandemic potential; Influenza B ‚Äî mainly human. Virus more stable in cool, low‚Äëhumidity conditions
  • Transmission: respiratory droplets/aerosols (small aerosols can reach lower airways); infectious ~1 day before to ~5‚Äì7 days after symptoms; fomites possible

Clinical manifestations

  • Incubation: 1‚Äì4 days
  • Typical: fever, chills, myalgia, headache, malaise, anorexia; sore throat, dry cough, nasal symptoms
  • Course: fever 3‚Äì5 days; cough and fatigue may persist 1‚Äì2+ weeks
  • Age notes: children ‚Äî more bronchiolitis/otitis; elderly ‚Äî atypical presentation, less fever, higher complication risk; pregnancy/chronic disease ‚Äî increased severity
  • Complications: primary viral pneumonia, secondary bacterial pneumonia
  • Warning signs: severe dyspnea or hypoxia, chest pain, hemoptysis, persistent high fever, altered mental status, dehydration, rapid deterioration or no improvement after 48‚Äì72 h

Treatment, Prevention, and Control

  • Antivirals
    • Neuraminidase inhibitors (oseltamivir, zanamivir): most effective if started within 48 h; prioritize severe/high‚Äërisk/hospitalized patients
    • Baloxavir: single‚Äëdose option; resistance can occur
    • Adamantanes (amantadine, rimantadine): not recommended due to widespread resistance
  • Vaccination
    • Annual vaccine (IIV, LAIV, recombinant), usually quadrivalent; routine ‚â•6 months, high priority for older adults, pregnant people, healthcare workers, and those with chronic conditions.

Treatment, Prevention, and Control

  • Infection control
    • Hand hygiene, masking, isolation/cohorting in healthcare
    • Antiviral prophylaxis for selected exposed high‚Äërisk individuals or to control institutional outbreaks
  • Surveillance
    • Ongoing monitoring of antigenic drift/shift and antiviral resistance to inform vaccine composition and treatment
